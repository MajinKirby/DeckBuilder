The Field

To understand how to play Yu-Gi-Oh!, you need to first know the field it’s played on. Below is what the current Yu-Gi-Oh! field looks like. Lets break it down.

Each square is called a zone, and each zone has its purpose.

Directly above the deck is the Graveyard, sometimes abbreviated as “GY”, which is best described as the discard pile, where monster cards go once destroyed by battle or card effect, and spell/trap cards go once their effects have resolved or they are destroyed.

The bottom left zone is where the Extra Deck goes.

Directly above the Extra Deck is where you play field spells.

The zones in between the Field Spell Zone and Graveyard Zone are the Monster Card Zones, sometimes known as the front row. This is where your monster cards are played.

Directly below the Monster Card Zones are the Spell & Trap Card Zones, sometimes known as the “back row”. This is where you play Trap cards and Spell cards that are not Field spells.

You will notice that the left and rightmost Spell & Trap Card Zones have a unique symbol in them. There are also two zones, above the Monster Card Zones. With the current catalog of cards we are using, these Zones and their purpose are irrelevant, so their purpose and functionality will be explained later.

Now that you know how the field is set up and what each zone is used for, it’s time to explain the cards themselves.

Monster Cards

At the top we have the card’s name, in this case, we have, “Buster Blader”.

Right next to the card’s name, is the card’s attribute. Buster Blader is an “Earth” attribute monster.

Right below the name and attribute, are some stars. These stars are the monster’s level. Buster Blader here has seven stars, meaning he’s a level 7 monster.

Right below the card artwork is a box with more information. In the top-left corner of the box is the card’s type. In this case, Buster Blader is a warrior type monster.

An important note is that beginners often confuse type and attribute with each other. An easy way to distinguish the two is:

When a Card's effect specifies an attribute, the name of the attribute will be in all caps.

For example, when a card says, “Banish one Light and one Dark Monster from your Graveyard.” The text in the effect will say. “Banish one LIGHT and one DARK Monster from your GY.”


Next to the monster’s type, there can be additional monster types. Most monsters will only ever have “Effect” written.

Should a monster have nothing written after the main type, then they are classified as Normal monsters. Normal monsters have text in the effect box, but this text has no impact on the game state. For example:

Dark Magician here is a normal monster with the flavor text, “The ultimate wizard in terms of attack and defense.”

Going back to Buster Blader, he has the effect, “Gains 500 ATK for each Dragon monster your opponent controls or is in their GY.”

Finally, at the very bottom of the card are the monster’s attack and defense stats, abbreviated as ATK and DEF.

Now, spell cards.

Spell Cards

Spell Cards are divided into 5 Categories, Normal, Continuous, Quick-Play, Field, and Ritual.

Normal Spells, like Raigeki, are simple to understand. you activate them from the hand, and once they resolve their effect, they are sent to the graveyard.

Continuous Spells, like Messenger of Peace, are played onto the field and remain there until they are destroyed.

An important thing to remember is that continuous spells can have their effects negated but still remain on the field.

Quick Play Spells are a little more complex, due to interacting with chain links and spell speeds. These two aspects will be explained later, but for quick-play spells, all you need to know is that unlike other spells, quick-play spells can be activated from your hand in response to another card effect, but only during your turn. You can only activate quick-play spells on your opponent’s turn if you set them face down on the field during your turn first.

Field Spells, like Umi, also function differently. as stated before, they are played in their own dedicated zone, where they function similarly to continuous spells, remaining on the field until they are destroyed.

Note that the effects of Umi are always present, affecting every designated monster at all times.

Finally, Ritual Spells, like Black Luster Ritual, are played in conjunction with ritual monsters. Each ritual spell shares a similar effect; to ritual summon their specific ritual monster, you tribute monsters from your hand/field whose combined LEVELS equal or exeed the level of the monster they are summoning.

Note: in order to ritual summon, you’ll need to have both a ritual spell and its designated ritual monster in your hand, unless the ritual spell states otherwise.

Trap Cards

Trap Cards are similar to spell cards.The key difference being that Trap Cards can not be activated directly from the hand. You must first set them facedown in the spell and trap card zone, and can not be activated on the same turn you set them.

Traps are divided into 3 categories: Normal, Counter and Continuous.

Normal Trap Cards are the most basic, you set them face down during your turn, then during any other turn you can flip them face up to activate their effect(s).

Counter Traps are similar to normal traps, except unlike normal traps, they can only be activated in response to certain actions being taken, and the cards that can be activated in response to them are other counter traps.

Finally, Continuous Traps are just like normal traps, but once activated they function exactly the same as continuous spells.

Now that we are done with the main deck, let’s quickly look at the extra deck.

The Extra Deck & Fusion Monsters

Fusion Monsters are nearly identical to monsters in the main deck, however they are purple, instead of yellow/brown. Also you’ll notice in the effect box, right next to the monster’s main type, is “Fusion”. this is what denotes a monster as a fusion monster.

As their name may suggest, Fusion Monsters are a combination of two or more separate monsters, known as the fusion material. In this case Flame Swordsman’s fusion materials are “Flame Manipulator” and “Masaki the Legendary Swordsman”.

In order to conduct a fusion summon you must have the required fusion material on the field and or in your hand, then you must play a spell card known as Polymerization.

Once you have successfully fusion summoned a monster to the field, the monster will function the same as any other monster on the field, being able to attack, change battle position, use its effect should it have one, and go to the Graveyard once destroyed.

Now that you know the cards, it’s time to build your deck.

Building Your Deck

A Yu-Gi-Oh! Deck is made up of a combination of main deck monsters, spells, and traps. Your main deck must have a minimum of 40 cards and maximum of 60 cards. The Extra Deck is always optional, but should you be playing one, it may contain a maximum of 15 cards. You are also allowed a side deck of up to 15 cards. Your side deck may contain cards from both the main deck and extra deck. We’ll explain the side deck will be later in the flow of the game. One thing to know is that in all three decks, you may only have up to three copies of one card.

Now that you’ve built your deck, it’s time to learn how a typical game goes.

How to Play (For real, this time!)

Once you have an opponent and have decided who goes first, you’ll start by drawing your opening hand of five cards. Once both players have their opening hands, and your life points are set to the starting value of 8000, the duel begins and the player going first begins their turn.

A turn in Yu-Gi-Oh! is divided into phases listed below.

First is the Draw Phase.

In the draw phase the player will simply draw the top card of their deck and add it to their hand. An important thing to note is that the player going first does not draw for their opening turn.

Next, the Standby Phase.

This phase is where certain card effects activate, as well as paying any maintenance costs for cards. You’ll know these effects are activated and costs are paid, in the standby phase, because the card will specifically state it.

After the standby phase, is the Main Phase 1.

This is where a majority of the primary gameplay occurs. In this phase you can normal/special or set summon monsters, activate/set spell and trap cards, and utilize monster effects. An important rule is that you can only normal summon one monster per turn.

Once you’ve made all the plays you’ve liked in the main phase, you can move onto the Battle Phase.

In the Battle Phase, you can attack with your monsters. When two monsters are battling, the monster with fewer attack points is destroyed, and the owner loses life points equal to the difference between the two monsters' attack points.

When attacking a defense position monster, if the attacking monster has greater attack points than the defending monster's defense points, the defending monster is destroyed, but its owner does not lose any life points.

Should the defending monster have greater defense points than the attacking monster’s attack points, neither monster is destroyed but the owner of the attacking monster loses life points equal to the difference between the defending monster's defense points and the attacking monster's attack points.

Finally, if your opponent has now monsters on their field, you can attack their life points directly causing them to lose points equal to your monster's full attack point value. Each monster can only attack once per battle phase, and you do not have to attack if you do not wish.

Once you’ve finished with the Battle Phase, you can now move to the Main Phase 2.

The Main Phase 2 functions the same as the Main Phase 1, and exists to allow you to set up for your opponent’s turn.

Finally, once you’ve finished the Main Phase 2, you can now move to the End Phase.

Like the standby phase, nothing happens in the end phase, unless a card effect states otherwise.

Once the End Phase ends, your turn is over and your opponent’s turn begins, going in the same order as your turn.

Now that you know how a turn in Yu-Gi-Oh! is conducted, how do you win?

There are two ways to win.

The most common way is by reducing your opponent’s life points to 0. The more uncommon way is known as “decking out your opponent.” This occurs when, during your opponent’s draw phase, they have no cards remaining in their deck.

Now that you know how to play Yu-Gi-Oh!, go assemble your deck, find an opponent, and prepare to duel!
